History & Description

Jeita Grotto is one of Lebanon’s most remarkable natural landmarks and a symbol of the country’s geological heritage. Located in the Nahr al-Kalb valley north of Beirut, the grotto consists of two interconnected limestone caves formed over millions of years by the erosion of rock through underground water.

The lower cave was first discovered in 1836 and later developed for visitors through a boat journey along an underground river. The upper cave, opened to the public in 1969, features impressive stalactites and stalagmites, including one of the world’s longest known stalactites. Together, these formations create a breathtaking natural cathedral beneath the earth’s surface.

Jeita Grotto is not only a tourist attraction but also an essential source of fresh water for nearby regions. Recognized internationally, it was once a finalist in the New 7 Wonders of Nature competition. Today, Jeita Grotto stands as a testament to the power of nature and the importance of preserving natural monuments for future generations.

Quick Facts

Location: Nahr al-Kalb Valley, Lebanon

Discovered: 1836

Type: Limestone cave system
